Research

Laying off an employee. Denying a promotion. Comforting a grieving colleague. On the surface, words seem inconsequential in these moments — no phrase can reverse a loss.

Yet what people say profoundly shapes workplace relationships, the decisions we make, the ideas we implement, and our resilience in the face of setbacks. From presidents addressing national tragedies to decision-makers trying to reduce organizational friction, I examine why communication unintentionally backfires, reveal paradoxes between what we think works and what actually does, and identify concrete behaviors that help us connect amidst adversity.

I draw on social psychological and micro-organizational behavior theory emphasizing the role of emotions, norms, and authenticity — with implications spanning organizational behavior, psychology, marketing, communication, and health.

Field test

What would you say?

A colleague just found out their grant application was rejected. They spent months on it. You want to say something supportive. Which lands better?

// from the researchMost people instinctively reach for Option A — the silver lining feels constructive and optimistic. But my ongoing work finds that sympathy expressions are less effective when they focus on the silver lining. Acknowledging the loss itself, before reframing it, is what makes people feel heard.
