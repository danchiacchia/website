Featured research

(Mis)alignment in sympathy expressions

What would you say?

A colleague returns to work after losing a parent. You want to say something supportive. Which lands better?

Most consolers instinctively reach for Option A — but this research finds validation (Option B) is what grievers typically need: a message that makes them feel understood. The nuance: when a loss threatens esteem — like losing a job — the pattern flips, and positive reframing becomes the more effective strategy.

Featured research

Lay theories of decision conflict and the role of the true self

Which one is the true self?

A colleague is conflicted about AI in the workplace. Their head points toward being pro-AI — but their automatic gut reaction is against it. Deep down, which do they truly prefer?

Here's the catch: your answer says as much about you as about them. This research finds that judgments of the true self are motivated — people locate another person's "true" preference on whichever side aligns with what they themselves believe is good or right. The true self is seen through an evaluative lens, not a neutral one.

About Daniel

Multi-method research on communication in hard moments

Daniel J. Chiacchia is a Postdoctoral Research Associate at the Center for Leadership in the Future of Work, University of Zürich. He received his PhD in Organizational Behaviour and Human Resource Management at the Rotman School of Management, University of Toronto. Drawing on social psychological and micro-organizational behavior theory, his research examines the role of emotions, norms, and authenticity in how we communicate through adversity — with implications spanning organizational behavior, psychology, marketing, communication, and health.

His multi-method approach combines qualitative interviews, lab and field experiments, archival analyses, and psychometric natural language processing to capture nuanced expression at scale. He is committed to open science: pre-registering studies, powering experiments appropriately, internally replicating effects, and making data, code, and stimuli publicly available.
